EB-1A Extraordinary Ability Green Card: Complete Guide, Process, and Steps

The EB-1A lets individuals with extraordinary ability self-petition for a green card — no employer and no labor certification required. Here's the full eligibility test, filing process, timeline, and costs.

The EB-1A (Employment-Based First Preference, Extraordinary Ability) visa is a U.S. immigrant category for individuals who have demonstrated extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics. It is designed for people at the very top of their field — those who can show sustained national or international acclaim and that their achievements have been recognized through extensive documentation.

Key benefits

  • You do not need a U.S. employer (you can self-petition).
  • No labor certification (PERM) is required.
  • Often faster than other employment-based categories.
  • Can lead directly to a green card (lawful permanent residence).

Eligibility Criteria (Legal Requirements)

To qualify, you must satisfy USCIS regulatory criteria under 8 CFR §204.5(h). You must show either (A) a one-time major achievement recognized internationally — e.g., a Nobel Prize, Olympic Medal, Oscar, Pulitzer, or similar — or (B) at least 3 of the following 10 criteria:

  • Nationally or internationally recognized prizes/awards for excellence.
  • Membership in associations that demand outstanding achievement.
  • Published material about you in major media or journals.
  • Participation as a judge of others' work (peer review, panelist, editorial board).
  • Original contributions of major significance in your field.
  • Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or media.
  • Display of your work in artistic exhibitions or showcases.
  • Leading or critical role for distinguished organizations.
  • High salary or remuneration compared to others in the field.
  • Commercial success in performing arts (box office, sales, etc.).

The Two-Part Test (USCIS analysis)

USCIS officers apply a two-step analysis: first, the Objective Evidence Test — have you met at least 3 of the 10 criteria (or one major award)? Second, the Final Merits Determination — do you truly rank among the small percentage at the top of your field, and does the evidence show sustained acclaim and future benefit to the U.S.?

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1. Gather and Prepare Evidence

  • A detailed CV/resume with accomplishments and career history.
  • Recommendation letters from experts in your field (U.S. and international).
  • Documentation proving you meet at least 3 of the regulatory criteria.
  • Proof that you will continue working in your field in the U.S.
  • Any relevant awards, publications, media articles, patents, judging invitations, etc.

Step 2. Prepare and File Form I-140 (Immigrant Petition for Alien Worker)

This is the core petition for EB-1A. You can file it yourself (self-petition) — no employer needed. Form: I-140. Fee: $715 (as of 2025; check current USCIS fee schedule). Processing time: 4–9 months (can use Premium Processing for a 15-business-day decision; fee ~$2,805).

Step 3. USCIS Review and Adjudication

You'll receive a Receipt Notice (Form I-797C) confirming the petition was received. Later, either a Request for Evidence (RFE) if USCIS needs more proof, or an Approval Notice (Form I-797A) if granted.

Family Members

  • Spouse and unmarried children under 21 can be included in the same application.
  • If abroad, they apply for immigrant visas as E-14/E-15 derivatives.
  • If in the U.S., they file their own I-485 concurrently or after approval.

What Happens After I-140 Approval?

A. If you are inside the U.S. — Adjustment of Status

You'll file Form I-485 (Application to Register Permanent Residence), either concurrently with the I-140 (if your priority date is current per the Visa Bulletin) or after I-140 approval.

  • Form I-485 + fee ($1,440 for adults, includes biometrics).
  • Form I-765 (work permit) and I-131 (advance parole) — optional but recommended.
  • Medical exam (Form I-693).
  • Passport, I-94, and all prior visa records.
  • 2 passport photos.
  • Evidence of lawful U.S. entry (I-94).

Once filed, you'll receive Receipt Notices (I-797C) for each form, a biometrics appointment notice, an EAD/AP card (within 3–6 months), and an interview notice (interviews are mostly waived for EB-1A). After adjudication, USCIS issues your green card if approved.

B. If you are outside the U.S. — Consular Processing

Once the I-140 is approved, USCIS forwards your case to the National Visa Center (NVC). You'll receive an NVC case number, pay the Immigrant Visa Fee and Affidavit of Support Fee (if applicable) online, complete Form DS-260 online, and upload civil documents (birth certificate, passport, police certificates, etc.). At the interview, bring your original documents, medical exam, passport, and approval notice. If approved, you receive an Immigrant Visa valid for about 6 months — enter the U.S. before it expires, and your green card will be mailed to your U.S. address within a few weeks.

After Approval

Whether through Adjustment of Status or Consular Processing, you become a Lawful Permanent Resident (LPR). You'll receive a green card (Form I-551) and a Permanent Resident Number (A-Number). You can later apply for U.S. citizenship after 5 years.
